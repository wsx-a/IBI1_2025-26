Reflection
This practical was more difficult than some of the previous ones because it combined probability, loops, arrays, and plotting in one task. At the beginning, I found it hard to understand how infection and recovery should be updated over time, especially in the stochastic model, because the result is different every time the code runs.
The first SIR model was the easiest part for me. It helped me understand the basic idea of dividing the population into susceptible, infected, and recovered groups. I also learned that the infection probability is not just beta, but beta multiplied by the proportion of infected people in the population.
The vaccination model was interesting because it showed clearly how increasing vaccination reduces the number of infected people. From the graph, I could see the idea of herd immunity more clearly than just reading about it in theory.
The spatial SIR model was the hardest part because I had to think about neighbours in a 2D grid and make sure the infection only spread to valid positions in the array. However, this was also the most interesting part, because the plots made the disease spread easier to imagine.
The most important thing I learned from this practical is that real-world biological problems can be turned into simple probabilistic models in Python. I also learned that even a simple model can produce useful and meaningful results.
